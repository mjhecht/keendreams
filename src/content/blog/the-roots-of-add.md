I'd like to write a blog post about ADD, which uses my own experiences as a lens that's helped me to understand what's really going on, here. I recently watched a YouTube video who has ADD, which talks about her task management practices: which stem from an awareness of energy, and attentiveness to what feels difficult or has cost energetically, versus what feels generative and replenishing. I resonate a lot with this set of ideas, and it explains my own self-noticings quite well. There's a moment where she mentions that responding to text or WhatsApp messages has a high energetic drag; she talks about context switching having a high cost, and she reflects on the "eating the frog" idea.

  

I've wanted to weave in self observation, in which it seems to me that ADD is a simplified label for what's clearly a multidimensional state of consciousness. For me, I've glimpsed how at root my own ADD is an emotional attachment issue, because I've caught my brain in heightened states devoting tremendous amounts of thought trying to "figure out" or "solve" what are fundamentally _relationship_ problems, where rumination overtakes useful cognition, and the purpose of that rumination is to try and find a way out of dysregulated nervous-system states: i.e., an attachment issue (relationship problem) is present, and I'm trying to think my way out of it, which doesn't work. The best I've been able to do in that case, which is why _writing_ is so adaptive for me, is to pop open a journal and capture that ruminative thought stream: when confined to my head, energy doesn't seem to move. When I type, there is a certain kind of detailed high-resolution thought which is able to really capture and integrate the dimensions of what is happening within, and move these thoughts forwards. What I observe at these points is that I'm engaging my motor cortex and _integrating_ that with the thoughts, so that in a way I'm "thinking with my body" through movements of fingers on keyboard, and that advances thought forwards by giving it shape in the world. Over the years, I've also developed a deeper relationship with writing by hand: it feels significantly slower than typing, and yet in that slowness, it forces my mind to slow down, and to actually capture less detail: that, in turn, yields higher "compression" where more "emotional" words come forth that can push the thinking circuits into more "heart-centered" circuits, naming feelings more. While this can be uncomfortable (e.g., the slowness of the writing might surface words like "frustration" more frequently), in fact, processing relational issues _needs_ contact with feelings like frustration, anger, and hurt — because we can't selectively numb. So I've come to love my Morning Pages practice as a space to work through feelings I'm not otherwise aware of, and come to clarity and orientation around my day.
