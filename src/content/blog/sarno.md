I was sick with a bad cold and had flu-like symptoms as well; at one point I coughed very hard and it felt like something popped out of place in my low back. This was the second or third time I’d pulled something there, which was much worse this time: When I tried to stand up, my whole lower body would spasm with pain, and all I could do was move out of that position and lay in bed. I later dragged myself in crab-like fashion to get a ride to a chiropractor’s office. In a couple weeks I was slowly hobbling around with a stick, and months later seemed to have recovered as well as I was going to: a knot in my back remaining that was aggravated by running, extended sitting, and other positions, and a feeling of fragility. Then I remembered that someone had recommended _Healing Back Pain_ years ago, so I ordered and began reading the book.

Dr. Sarno’s theory is essentially that back pain in many people is associated with a mechanism of emotional repression, particularly for anger and rage, which some learn in childhood are not appropriate expressions. E.g., if there is a parental withdrawal of love when we show frustration, we very effectively clamp down so as not to show it; here I am synthesizing a bit of material from _The Drama of the Gifted Child_ by Alice Miller. Sarno calls the mechanism TMS, by which certain muscles are mildly deprived of oxygen, which can result in severe pain, but which is physically harmless. Most interestingly, simply knowing about and accepting that this mechanism is active is enough to stop it from happening.

In other words, the psyche creates an elaborate “ruse” by which pain/worry is used to cover up or distract from “inappropriate” emotions, for as vulnerable kids, we might lose life-giving love through acting out.

I found that reading the book was incredibly important: in order to switch to a new paradigm (kind of like a Copernican shift!) we are helped by the weight of an authoritative voice, who has seen thousands of patients manifest the pattern he describes; otherwise, our medical conditioning (that it’s a physical injury, weakness, defect) holds influence.

As I read, I meditated on the idea of the book: that my back pain must be covering up emotions underneath. I kept instructing my psyche: “don’t do this! Let me feel these feelings, even if they are uncomfortable.” I had to see through an elaborately-imagined image, constructed from what a chiropractor told me: that one of my discs was weak, brittle, liable to easily burst, and that it would be a lifelong problem. Sarno also advocates not being particularly tender with oneself, because it’s not a structural issue. So I went out running, too.

After three days of reading and meditating on the message I was sending to my psyche, the knot and discomfort in my low back had vanished. I felt a miraculous sense of limberness, and felt I’d been given a new lease on life.

Many people, according to the book, will need psychotherapy to get at the underlying repressed emotions. He advocates a specific kind of therapy. I was grateful that for me, simply reading and working to shift my inner model was so effective. It felt like the book itself was medicinal, and that in a sense reading it triggered the solution to my problem.
